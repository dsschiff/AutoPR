AI education isn't one-size-fits-all 🎯

Our study of 1,465 students across US, UK & Germany reveals 3 distinct student profiles when it comes to AI confidence and engagement.

Results suggest we need tailored approaches 👇🧵

[LINK]

@GRAIL_center @purduepolsci

---

We find that AI self-efficacy (confidence in using AI) depends on more than just knowledge 📚

Interest acts as the bridge connecting attitudes, literacy, and actual AI usage.

Simply using tools like ChatGPT boosts student interest ⬆️

---

Using advanced modeling, we identified 3 student profiles:

🚀 AI Advocates (48%): Tech-savvy, confident
⚠️ Cautious Critics (21%): Skeptical, low confidence  
🤔 Pragmatic Observers (31%): Neutral, moderate interest

Each needs different educational approaches

[Attach image: img_1.jpg]

---

Demographics matter for AI engagement 📊

• Advocates: mostly male, STEM backgrounds
• Critics: overrepresented in humanities, predominantly female
• Access to AI education varies widely—Critics report least exposure

This has implications for equity

---

Our path model shows positive attitudes predict higher AI engagement, while negative attitudes barely influence behavior 🔍

This suggests focusing on building interest and positive experiences rather than just addressing concerns

[Attach image: img_0.jpg]

---

For educators and governance teams: AI programs need tailoring 🎯

• Advocates: encourage critical thinking about ethics
• Critics: demystify AI, show relevance to non-tech fields
• Observers: use hands-on experiences to spark engagement

---

If we don't address these different profiles, we risk leaving entire groups behind in an AI-driven world 🌍
