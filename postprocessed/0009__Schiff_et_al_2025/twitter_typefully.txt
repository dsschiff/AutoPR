Are we failing to teach social responsibility in STEM? 🧵

New 5-year study of 124 Georgia Tech students reveals a troubling pattern: undergrads leave college LESS connected to societal impact than when they started.

[LINK]

@GRAIL_center @purduepolsci 👇

---

📊 Researchers tracked students across 3 dimensions of social responsibility:

• Personal Social Awareness (ethical awareness)
• Professional Development (career preparedness) 
• Professional Connectedness (feeling tied to societal impact)

Results were eye-opening...

[Attach image: img_0.jpg]

---

🔻 Professional Connectedness DROPPED significantly from 5.65 to 5.43 by graduation (p < 0.001)

• Students increasingly prioritized salary over societal impact
• Self-efficacy to drive social change declined

STEM education isn't fostering social responsibility

[Attach image: img_1.jpg]

---

💡 But here's the twist: students DID grow in social awareness—just not from their STEM classes

The biggest drivers of growth were INFORMAL activities:
• Peer discussions
• Family conversations  
• Self-guided exploration

Formal education barely moved the needle

---

❌ Students described their STEM courses as:

• Too technical (little ethics integration)
• Disconnected from societal dimensions
• Career-focused but not impact-focused

Institutional social responsibility initiatives felt rare and uninspiring

[Attach image: img_3.jpg]

---

⚠️ Why this matters: STEM professionals shape our future

From AI to climate tech, their decisions affect entire societies. If we're not embedding social responsibility in the STEM pipeline, how can we expect future leaders to prioritize it?

---

🔧 The fix based on study findings:

• Integrate ethics into core STEM curricula
• Leverage peer-based learning (proven most impactful)
• Provide real-world challenges via internships/capstone projects

We need holistic STEM education, not just technical training
