Are we failing to teach social responsibility in STEM? A concerning 5-year longitudinal study suggests we might be. [LINK]

➤ Researchers followed 124 Georgia Tech STEM students and found that Professional Connectedness—students' sense of connection to societal impact—dropped significantly from 5.65 to 5.43 by graduation (p < 0.001).

✅ Key findings:
• Students increasingly prioritized salary over societal impact as career motivators
• Self-efficacy to drive social change declined over time
• Growth in social awareness came from informal activities (peer discussions, family conversations) rather than formal STEM education

➤ Students described their STEM courses as too technical, disconnected from societal dimensions, and focused on job readiness rather than societal impact.

• This matters because STEM professionals shape critical decisions affecting entire societies—from AI development to climate technology.

✅ The study suggests we need to:
• Integrate ethics and societal impact into core STEM curricula
• Leverage peer-based learning approaches
• Provide real-world challenges through internships and capstone projects

We can no longer treat STEM education as purely technical training. The next generation of scientists and engineers needs both technical rigor AND social responsibility.
