Ever felt like your organization is a battlefield of hidden alliances and clashing interests? New research introduces Subcoalition Cluster Analysis (SCA)—a computational method to uncover the invisible factions driving organizational conflict. [LINK]

Organizations aren't monolithic entities. They're arenas of competing stakeholder goals where teams form factions—subcoalitions—with aligned preferences. These fault lines drive decision-making chaos, yet most management models treat organizations as unified.

➤ The breakthrough: SCA maps hidden organizational divisions using preference-based clustering that groups actors by maximizing shared utility

➤ Real-world validation: Wikipedia's 2013 Visual Editor rollout revealed two warring subcoalitions (technical vs. cultural focus), explaining why the initiative sparked rebellion from veteran editors

➤ Practical applications: Baseball Hall of Fame voting analysis (2009-2016) uncovered persistent rifts between pro-PED forgiveness and anti-PED purist factions

Results suggest SCA can help managers identify internal conflicts before they explode, tailor strategies to real divisions rather than assumptions, and avoid decision paralysis caused by hidden factional wars.

The method is computationally practical—handling incomplete data, adapting across industries, and running fast enough for real-world applications.

For researchers and organizational leaders studying conflict dynamics, this offers new insights into how factions form and persist within institutions.
