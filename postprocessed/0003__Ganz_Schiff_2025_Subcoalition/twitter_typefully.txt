Organizations aren't monolithic. They're arenas of competing stakeholder goals. Yet most management models treat them as unified entities.

Reality? Teams form factions—subcoalitions—with aligned preferences. These fault lines drive decision-making chaos.

---

The problem? Subcoalitions are hard to observe and harder to model. Machine learning struggles with incomplete data and group dynamics complexity.

Enter SCA: A computational breakthrough that maps hidden organizational fault lines.

[Attach image: img_0.jpg]

---

How does SCA work? Think preference-based clustering that groups actors by maximizing shared utility.

✅ Initialization: Seeds meaningful coalitions
✅ Renegotiation: Fine-tunes alignment
✅ Stability: Stops when no one wants to defect

---

Case study: Wikipedia's 2013 Visual Editor rollout sparked rebellion from veteran editors.

SCA revealed two warring subcoalitions:
🛠️ Technical design focus
🌍 Cultural norms focus

Leadership missed these divisions—leading to strategy disaster.

Baseball Hall of Fame voting (2009-2016): Writers debated inducting players linked to PEDs.

SCA uncovered the rift:
⚾ Pro-PED forgiveness
⚾ Anti-PED purists

This schism grew, reinforcing decision-making silos over time.
