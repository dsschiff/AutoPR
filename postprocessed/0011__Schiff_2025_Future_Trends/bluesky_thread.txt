AI governance faces fundamental tensions: centralized vs. decentralized control, robust vs. minimal enforcement, adaptive vs. enduring rules. My new work explores how aviation, climate, and open-source models might guide us 🧵 [LINK]

---

The challenge: balancing competing forces in AI regulation

• Centralized vs. decentralized control
• Robust vs. minimal enforcement
• Adaptive vs. enduring rules  
• Private leadership vs. shared governance
• Narrow vs. broad societal goals

[Attach image: img_0.jpg]

---

I examine 4 governance models for lessons:

✈️ Aviation: Centralized, safety-first, costly
🌱 Organic food: Decentralized, private-driven, inconsistent
🌍 Climate: Shared, flexible, weak enforcement
💻 Open-source: Collaborative, adaptive, fragile

[Attach image: img_1.jpg]

---

Today's reality: fragmentation dominates. EU centralizing with AI Act; US going decentralized. Monitoring varies from strict compliance to voluntary audits. Tech firms lead while public input lags

---

The wildcard: AI's breakneck pace. Generative AI blindsided policymakers. A crisis—like deepfake election interference—could trigger rapid regulation. Public accountability demands intensify

---

The way forward: adaptive, hybrid models combining centralized safety oversight (aviation-style), decentralized innovation (open-source), and broad societal goals (climate-style). Experimentation essential

---

For policy practitioners: What trade-offs matter most? Centralization or flexibility? Private leadership or public control? AI governance shapes society itself—the stakes couldn't be higher
