AI governance is pulling us in 5 directions at once: centralized vs. decentralized, robust vs. minimal enforcement, adaptive vs. enduring rules. My new work explores how aviation, climate, and open-source models might guide us through this complexity 🧵 [LINK]

---

The challenge: balancing competing tensions in AI regulation

• Centralized vs. decentralized control
• Robust vs. minimal enforcement  
• Adaptive vs. enduring rules
• Private leadership vs. shared governance
• Narrow vs. broad societal goals

[Attach image: img_0.jpg]

---

I adapt lessons from 4 existing governance models:

✈️ Aviation: Centralized, safety-first, but costly
🌱 Organic food: Decentralized, private-driven, inconsistent
🌍 Climate: Shared, flexible, but weak enforcement
💻 Open-source: Collaborative, adaptive, but fragile

[Attach image: img_1.jpg]

---

Current reality: fragmentation everywhere

• EU centralizing with AI Act; US taking decentralized approach
• Monitoring ranges from strict compliance to voluntary audits
• Tech firms dominate decisions while public input gets sidelined

---

The wildcard? AI's pace itself. Generative AI caught policymakers off guard. A major crisis—deepfake election scandal—could force rapid regulation. Meanwhile, public demand for accountability grows 📈

---

The path forward suggests adaptive, hybrid models combining:

• Centralized safety oversight (aviation-style)
• Decentralized innovation hubs (open-source style)
• Broad societal goals (climate-style)

Experimentation will be key 🔬

---

Why this matters: AI governance shapes society itself. Done right, it addresses inequality and climate impact. Done wrong, it entrenches power imbalances and amplifies harms. The stakes are enormous

---

For policy practitioners and governance teams: What trade-offs would you prioritize? Centralization or flexibility? Private leadership or public control?
