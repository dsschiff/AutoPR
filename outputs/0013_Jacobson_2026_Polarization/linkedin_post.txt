How did AI policymaking in the U.S. shift from bipartisan collaboration to partisan gridlock in just a few years? Our new research in Policy & Internet tracked this evolution across 44 states from 2019-2022, revealing surprising insights into the politics of emerging technology governance.

https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/poi3.70020

✅ The opportunity: AI policy began as a rare blank slate with no entrenched partisan playbooks. Over 150 AI-related bills emerged across states, initially focused on collaborative approaches to transparency, privacy, and ethics.

➤ Early bipartisan wins centered on "soft governance" tools—transparency requirements and research initiatives that avoided polarizing debates. Illinois passed hiring algorithm disclosure laws, while Idaho framed pretrial risk assessments as governance reform rather than criminal justice issues.

• But by 2022, we observed four key triggers that fractured this cooperation:
• Competing problem definitions (broad transparency vs. equity-focused reforms)
• Divergent policy tools (soft notifications vs. hard demographic audits)
• Shifting stakeholder dynamics (academic neutrality vs. private sector lobbying)
• Strategic "subsystem shopping" (framing issues to navigate partisan terrain)

➤ The pattern reveals how Republicans gravitated toward pro-AI positions in defense and law enforcement, while Democrats focused on risk mitigation and equity concerns—mirroring broader ideological divides over regulation.

For governance teams and policy practitioners: Results suggest sustaining collaboration requires framing AI around transparency and innovation, starting with soft governance approaches, and leveraging nonpartisan expertise to mediate debates.

The dynamics shaping AI policy today will influence how we govern other emerging technologies like biotech and quantum computing. What strategies are you seeing work for maintaining bipartisan cooperation in tech policy?
