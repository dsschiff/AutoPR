🚨 How did AI policymaking in the U.S. go from bipartisan collaboration to partisan gridlock in just a few years? The story reveals surprising insights into politics, technology, and strategy. Let’s dive in 🧵:

---

1/ 🎯 **The Question**:  
Can emerging technologies like AI avoid the hyper-partisanship that dominates U.S. politics? Between 2019–2022, state-level AI policymaking started as a rare bipartisan effort—but partisan divides quickly took root. Why?  

---

2/ 🧐 **The Context**:  
AI policymaking began as a blank slate. With no entrenched partisan playbooks, legislators tackled issues like transparency, privacy, and ethics collaboratively. Over 150 AI-related bills were introduced across 44 states during this period.  

---

3/ 💡 **Early Bipartisan Wins**:  
Legislators focused on "soft governance" tools—like transparency requirements and research initiatives—to avoid polarizing debates. For example:  
- **Illinois (2019)**: Hiring algorithms legislation focused on consent, not demographics.  
- **Idaho**: Pretrial risk assessments framed as governance, not criminal justice reform.

---

4/ 🤔 **But Polarization Lurks**:  
By 2022, bipartisan windows narrowed. Why? The research identifies **4 key "triggers"** of polarization in AI policy:  
1️⃣ Competing problem definitions  
2️⃣ Divergent policy tools  
3️⃣ Stakeholder dynamics  
4️⃣ Strategic issue framing ("subsystem shopping")  

---

5/ 🔑 **Trigger 1 – Problem Definitions**:  
Early on, AI was framed broadly: transparency, privacy, ethics. But when linked to issues like racial equity or economic redistribution, partisan divides flared.  
- Democrats: Equity-focused AI reforms.  
- Republicans: Industry self-regulation.  

![Figure 1](./img/img_1.jpg)  

---

6/ 🔧 **Trigger 2 – Policy Tools**:  
Consensus around "soft" tools (e.g., notifications, research) gave way to conflict over "hard" tools (e.g., demographic audits, stricter regulation).  
- Republicans: Innovation-friendly, minimal intervention.  
- Democrats: Robust oversight, data accountability.  

---

7/ 🤝 **Trigger 3 – Stakeholders**:  
Academic experts initially fostered neutrality. But as private-sector lobbying ramped up, partisan dynamics intensified.  
- Example: Bail insurers in Idaho backed bipartisan regulation.  
- In Colorado, fragmented AI lobbying allowed early consensus on facial recognition.  

![Figure 2](./img/img_2.jpg)  

---

8/ 🎯 **Trigger 4 – Subsystem Shopping**:  
Policy entrepreneurs strategically framed AI issues to sidestep polarization.  
- Example: Colorado facial recognition debates combined education & law enforcement frameworks to avoid criminal justice conflicts.  

But as AI became tied to party-owned domains (e.g., defense for Republicans, equity for Democrats), polarization hardened.  

---

9/ 🚨 **Partisan Fragmentation**:  
By 2022, partisan cleavages were clear:  
- Republicans: Pro-AI in defense, law enforcement.  
- Democrats: Focused on risks, equity.  

This mirrored broader ideological divides over regulation and government intervention.  

![Figure 3](./img/img_3.jpg)  

---

10/ 🧠 **Introducing "Subsystem Shopping"**:  
The study coins a new term: **subsystem shopping**—strategically framing issues to align with favorable, less polarized policy domains.  
This approach worked for early bipartisan AI governance but faltered as partisan cues solidified.  

---

11/ 🚀 **Lessons for Future AI Policy**:  
To sustain collaboration, policymakers must:  
1️⃣ Frame AI around transparency & innovation (not polarizing equity issues).  
2️⃣ Start with "soft" governance to build consensus.  
3️⃣ Leverage nonpartisan experts to mediate debates.  
4️⃣ Use subsystem shopping to navigate contentious policy terrains.  

---

12/ 🌍 **Why It Matters**:  
AI governance isn’t just a tech issue—it’s a political one. The dynamics shaping AI policy today will influence how we govern other emerging tech, like biotech and quantum computing.
