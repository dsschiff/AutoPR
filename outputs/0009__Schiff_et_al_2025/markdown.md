🚨 **Are we failing to teach social responsibility in STEM?** 🚨  
A 5-year study at Georgia Tech reveals a shocking truth: most STEM undergrads leave college *less* connected to societal impact than when they started. Here’s what the data says—and how we can fix it. 🧵  

---

1/ **The Problem: STEM education isn’t preparing students for socially responsible careers.**  

Despite the critical role of STEM in addressing global challenges, undergrad attitudes toward *social responsibility (SR)* stagnate—or even worsen—over time.  

Why? Let’s dive in.  

---

2/ Researchers Schiff, Lee, Borenstein, and Zegura followed 124 STEM students over 5 years, using surveys & interviews to measure SR across 3 dimensions:  
- **Personal Social Awareness (PSA)**: Ethical awareness of societal issues.  
- **Professional Development (PD)**: Preparedness & identity as a professional.  
- **Professional Connectedness (PROC)**: Feeling tied to societal impact in their work.  

![Figure 0](./img/img_0.jpg)  

---

3/ **The Results (and they’re not pretty):**  
- **Professional Connectedness collapsed**: Scores dropped significantly from 5.65 to 5.43 by graduation (**p < 0.001**).  
- Students prioritized **salary** over societal impact as a career motivator.  
- Self-efficacy to drive social change? Declined.  

![Figure 1](./img/img_1.jpg)  

---

4/ So, is it all bad news? Not entirely.  

Students *did* report some growth in **social awareness**—but here's the catch:  
It wasn’t because of their STEM classes.  

Instead, the biggest drivers of SR growth were **informal** activities:  
- Peer discussions  
- Family conversations  
- Self-guided exploration  

![Figure 2](./img/img_2.jpg)  

---

5/ On the flip side, **formal STEM education barely moved the needle.**  

Students described their STEM courses as:  
- **Too technical**: Little integration of ethics or societal dimensions.  
- **Disconnected**: Institutional SR initiatives felt rare & uninspiring.  
- **Career-centric**: Focused more on job readiness than societal impact.  

![Figure 3](./img/img_3.jpg)  

---

6/ **Why does this matter?**  

STEM professionals *shape the future.*  
From AI to climate tech, their decisions can uplift—or harm—entire societies.  

But if we’re not embedding social responsibility into the STEM pipeline, how can we expect future leaders to prioritize it?  

---

7/ **The Fix:** Based on the study, here’s what needs to change:  
- Integrate **ethics and societal impact** into core STEM curricula.  
- Leverage **peer-based learning**: Discussions with friends proved hugely impactful.  
- Provide real-world challenges via **internships** and **capstone projects** linked to SR.  

![Figure 4](./img/img_4.jpg)  

---

8/ **A wake-up call for policymakers and educators:**  

STEM education can no longer be just about technical rigor. We need a holistic approach that fosters **social responsibility**—not just job readiness.  

Are we ready to rethink how we teach the next generation of scientists & engineers?
