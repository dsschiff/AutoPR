Are we failing to teach social responsibility in STEM? 🚨

New 5-year study of 124 Georgia Tech students reveals a troubling pattern: most STEM undergrads leave college LESS connected to societal impact than when they started.

https://doi.org/10.1186/s40594-025-00553-3 🧵

---

📊 The data tells a stark story:

• Professional Connectedness scores dropped significantly (5.65 → 5.43, p < 0.001)
• Students prioritized salary over societal impact
• Self-efficacy to drive social change declined

Published in International Journal of STEM Education

[Attach image: img_1.jpg]

---

But here's the twist: Students DID grow in social awareness—just not from their STEM classes 📚

The biggest drivers of responsibility growth were INFORMAL activities:
• Peer discussions
• Family conversations
• Self-guided exploration

---

Meanwhile, formal STEM education barely moved the needle 🎯

Students described their courses as:
• Too technical (little ethics integration)
• Disconnected from societal dimensions
• Career-focused rather than impact-focused

[Attach image: img_3.jpg]

---

Why this matters: STEM professionals shape our future—from AI to climate tech ⚡

If we're not embedding social responsibility into the STEM pipeline, how can we expect future leaders to prioritize societal impact over pure technical advancement?

---

The path forward 🛤️

We need to:
• Integrate ethics into core STEM curricula
• Leverage peer-based learning (it works!)
• Connect coursework to real-world challenges
• Rethink what "STEM readiness" really means

---

For policy practitioners & educators: STEM education can't just be technical training.

We need approaches that develop both competence AND conscience.
