1/ 🚨 Did you know? AI is helping radiologists detect diseases faster and more accurately than ever—but the governance frameworks guiding its use are often stuck in the "Wild West." 🏜️ Here's what’s happening—and why clinicians are stepping up to lead the charge: 🧵

2/ The adoption of AI tools in radiology is exploding in both the US and UK, driven by powerful neural networks and electronic health records. But this rapid progress unveils major challenges: accountability for AI errors, inadequate governance, and the rise of “moral crumple zones.”  

3/ What’s a "moral crumple zone"? It’s when clinicians are unfairly held accountable for AI errors they have *no control over*, like vendor-built algorithms misdiagnosing on clinical data. A frustrating consequence of underdeveloped governance mechanisms.  

4/ To investigate, researchers Bhargavi Ganesh, Daniel S. Schiff, and Stuart Anderson interviewed 22 experts—radiologists, oncologists, regulators, and more—to map modern AI governance in radiology. They uncovered striking gaps between regulation and reality.

6/ Yet, there’s vast *disparity* in how hospitals implement governance, largely depending on:  
- Resources 🏥  
- Technical expertise 🤖  
- Local population needs 🌍  

One hospital might meticulously validate AI with local data, while another relies on off-the-shelf algorithms with no safeguards.  

7/ Clinicians are stepping up as “governance entrepreneurs,” forming grassroots committees to monitor AI performance and develop institutional guidelines. But these efforts are usually underfunded, voluntary, and exist within rigid healthcare hierarchies.  

8/ Take a look at the team leading the charge: radiologists, oncologists, researchers, and even former regulators are coming together (often informally) to craft local AI solutions. Their diverse perspectives are essential to address the high stakes of clinical AI.  

[FIGURE_PLACEHOLDER_1 – Participant roles (table): radiologists, radiation oncologists, and others ensuring diverse expertise in governance efforts.]  

9/ Yet even these grassroots efforts face obstacles. Key issues include:  
1️⃣ Limited AI literacy among hospital staff 🚩  
2️⃣ Undefined standards for evaluating AI before and after deployment 📉  
3️⃣ Tensions between top-down mandates (e.g., FDA) and on-the-ground realities 🏗️  

10/ For example, some study participants revealed that hospitals deploy algorithms *without testing them* on their own patient data, exposing patients to AI "drift"—when AI systems degrade in accuracy over time.  

11/ Another finding: clinicians want streamlined policies. Right now, they’re overwhelmed by *contradictory guidelines* from regulators and professional societies. This “wild west” approach frustrates efforts to ensure that AI benefits patients safely and equitably.  

12/ So, what’s next? The researchers propose a **hybrid governance model**:  
1️⃣ **Standardized national guidelines** to ensure baseline safeguards.  
2️⃣ **Localized oversight** for customization to unique patient populations.  
3️⃣ **Funds for underserved hospitals** to build governance expertise.  

13/ Training is critical, too. Many clinicians lack AI literacy, leading to blind trust in vendor claims or over-reliance on algorithm outputs. Educating staff could close this gap and empower them to ask tough questions about AI tools.  

14/ Centralized monitoring systems—think "AI check-ups"—can also help track model performance over time, detecting issues like bias or drift early. Imagine an FDA-like body, but for real-time AI safety evaluation. 👩‍⚕️🤖  

15/ Governance isn’t just a regulatory issue—it’s about reducing disparities between well-resourced hospitals and smaller institutions. Equity in governance will ensure both types of facilities can benefit *and safeguard* AI advancements.

16/ TL;DR: AI in radiology is revolutionary, but its integration into healthcare is a double-edged sword. Clinicians are demanding smarter, fairer governance that:  
✔️ Protects patient safety,  
✔️ Shares best practices across hospitals, and  
✔️ Supports those who step up to lead.
