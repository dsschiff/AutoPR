1/ 🚨 AI governance is in chaos. Over 500 fragmented standards, overlapping frameworks, and contradictory regulations make it a maze. But there’s hope: a roadmap to harmonize this mess and create truly *human-centered* AI governance. Let’s unpack it: 🧵

2/ 🤔 **The Problem**: AI is advancing at breakneck speed, but our governance tools—laws, standards, certifications—are stuck in a fragmented, lagging system.  
Key challenges:  
- Redundancy & overlap  
- Confusion & paralysis  
- Ethical concepts lost in translation  
- Geopolitical divides  

![Figure 0](./img/img_0.jpg)

3/ **Challenge 1**: Translating concepts like fairness, transparency, and well-being into actionable standards is HARD.  
Why? These values are context-dependent and contested. Standards risk oversimplifying or "ethics-washing" these ideas, leaving structural inequities unaddressed.  

4/ **Challenge 2**: Voluntary standards dominate the landscape.  
Without enforcement mechanisms, organizations can cherry-pick compliance. This creates a culture of superficial audits and reputational signaling instead of meaningful accountability.  

5/ **Challenge 3**: The "15 competing standards" problem.  
The proliferation of AI standards leads to decision paralysis. Organizations face a patchwork of frameworks without clear guidance on which to prioritize or how to integrate them.  

![Figure 1](./img/img_1.jpg)

6/ **Challenge 4**: The pacing problem. AI evolves faster than governance structures.  
Standards take years to develop, but AI risks emerge in months. The result? Obsolete or irrelevant standards that fail to keep up with technological and societal shifts.  

7/ **Challenge 5**: Geopolitical fragmentation.  
Regions like the EU, US, and China pursue divergent AI regulations. Add cultural and institutional differences, and global alignment on governance becomes nearly impossible.  

8/ 🚀 **The Solution**: A human-centered, harmonized, and adaptive AI governance system.  
Here’s the proposed roadmap:  

First, harmonize standards through global collaboration among organizations like ISO, IEEE, and OECD. Use tools like crosswalks to map overlaps and reduce redundancy.  

![Figure 2](./img/img_2.jpg)

9/ Second, adopt a *layered approach* to governance.  
Start with high-level frameworks (e.g., NIST AI RMF), then build domain-specific guidelines (e.g., IEEE 7010 for well-being) and operational tools (e.g., model cards). This ensures scalability and adaptability.  

10/ Third, make governance agile.  
- Use **living documents** that evolve with AI tech.  
- Create **rapid-response taskforces** for real-time updates.  
- Experiment with **regulatory sandboxes** to test compliance frameworks in controlled environments.  

11/ Fourth, strengthen auditing ecosystems.  
- Train independent accreditation bodies to certify standards.  
- Promote transparency by encouraging organizations to publish audit results.  
- Use practical tools like datasheets and checklists to bridge the gap between principles and practice.  

![Figure 3](./img/img_3.jpg)

12/ Finally, embed stakeholder voices in every step.  
From marginalized communities to civil society and the Global South, diverse perspectives must guide governance decisions. Inclusive councils and citizen assemblies can ensure AI works for *everyone*.  

![Figure 4](./img/img_4.jpg)

13/ **What’s at stake?**  
Harmonized, human-centered governance could transform AI into a trustworthy tool for societal good. It can reduce compliance burdens, empower smaller organizations, and prioritize human dignity over technical metrics.  

14/ But the risks of inaction are huge: fragmented systems, ethics-washing, and widening inequities. The time to fix AI governance is NOW. What strategies resonate with you? How do you envision global collaboration on this front?  

15/ Let’s ensure AI works *for* humanity, not against it. 🌍
