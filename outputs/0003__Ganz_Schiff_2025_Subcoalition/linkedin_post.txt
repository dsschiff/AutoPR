Organizations aren't unified entities—they're arenas of competing stakeholder goals and hidden factions. Our new research in Management Science introduces Subcoalition Cluster Analysis (SCA), a computational method to uncover the invisible coalitions driving organizational conflict. https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2020.00013

Most management models treat organizations as monolithic, but reality is messier. Teams form subcoalitions with aligned preferences, creating fault lines that drive decision-making chaos. These divisions are hard to observe and even harder to model with traditional methods.

➤ SCA works like a smarter, preference-based clustering algorithm, grouping organizational actors by maximizing shared utility
➤ Unlike traditional methods that assume external factors drive preferences, SCA shows how coalitions form organically from shared goals
➤ Built for messy real-world data with incomplete information

Two compelling case studies demonstrate SCA's power:

• Wikipedia's 2013 Visual Editor rollout sparked rebellion from veteran editors. SCA revealed two warring subcoalitions—one focused on technical design, another on cultural norms. Leadership missed these divisions, leading to strategic disaster.

• Baseball Hall of Fame voting (2009-2016) on players linked to PEDs. SCA uncovered a growing rift between pro-PED forgiveness and anti-PED purist coalitions, showing how schisms reinforced decision-making silos over time.

For managers and governance practitioners, SCA offers practical benefits: identify and anticipate internal conflicts, tailor strategies to real divisions rather than assumptions, and avoid paralysis from hidden factional wars.
