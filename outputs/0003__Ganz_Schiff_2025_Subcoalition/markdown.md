1/ **Ever felt like your organization is a battlefield of hidden alliances and clashing interests?**  
A groundbreaking paper introduces **Subcoalition Cluster Analysis (SCA)**, a method to uncover the *invisible factions* driving organizational conflict. Here’s how it works—and why it matters: 🧵  

2/ Organizations aren’t monolithic. They’re arenas of competing stakeholder goals. Yet, most management models treat them as *unified entities*.  
Reality? Teams form factions—subcoalitions—with *aligned preferences*. These fault lines drive decision-making chaos.  

3/ The problem? Subcoalitions are **hard to observe** and even harder to model. Machine learning struggles with incomplete data and the sheer complexity of group dynamics.  
Enter **Subcoalition Cluster Analysis (SCA)**: A computational breakthrough that maps hidden fault lines.  

4/ **How does SCA work?**  
Think of it as a smarter, preference-based k-means algorithm. It groups organizational actors into subcoalitions by maximizing shared utility.  
The result? A clear map of internal divisions, even with sparse or messy data.  

📊 ![Figure 0](./img/img_0.jpg)

5/ SCA doesn’t just cluster—it explains conflict. Here’s how:  
1️⃣ **Initialization**: Seeds meaningful starting coalitions.  
2️⃣ **Renegotiation**: Fine-tunes groups to maximize preference alignment.  
3️⃣ **Stability**: Stops when no one wants to “defect.”  
Result? Stable, representative subcoalitions.  

6/ **Why is this a big deal?**  
Most clustering methods (like Latent Class Analysis) assume external factors drive preferences. SCA flips the script: coalitions form *organically* from shared goals.  
It’s bottom-up, relational, and built for messy real-world data.  

7/ **Case Study 1: Wikipedia Editor Wars**  
In 2013, Wikipedia rolled out a “Visual Editor” to attract new users. But it backfired—sparking rebellion from veteran editors.  
SCA revealed *two warring subcoalitions*:  

🛠️ One focused on technical design.  
🌍 Another on cultural norms.

8/ Wikipedia’s leadership missed these latent divisions, leading to a PR and strategy disaster.  
SCA didn’t just map the conflict—it showed *why* decisions alienated some editors while empowering others.  

9/ **Case Study 2: Baseball Hall of Fame Voting**  
From 2009-2016, baseball writers debated whether to induct players linked to performance-enhancing drugs (PEDs).  
SCA uncovered a rift:  

⚾ Pro-PED forgiveness.  
⚾ Anti-PED purists.  

This schism grew, reinforcing decision-making silos over time.

10/ **What’s the big takeaway?**  
SCA turns organizational chaos into clarity. Managers can:  
✅ Identify & anticipate internal conflicts.  
✅ Tailor strategies to *real* divisions, not assumptions.  
✅ Avoid decision paralysis caused by hidden factional wars.  

11/ But it’s not just theory. SCA is computationally **practical**:  
💻 Handles incomplete data.  
📈 Adapts across industries (e.g., editorial disputes, voting patterns).  
⚡ Fast enough for real-world applications.
