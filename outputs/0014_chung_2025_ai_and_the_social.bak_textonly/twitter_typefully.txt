Can 350+ years of political philosophy help us govern AI? 🤔

Our new paper explores how social contract theories apply to algorithmic decision-making in welfare, policing, and hiring.

Published in AIES 2025 👇

https://doi.org/10.1609/aies.v8i1.36575 🧵

---

The challenge: AI systems now make decisions that affect millions, but they weren't designed with democratic legitimacy in mind.

Transparency gaps, algorithmic bias, and global reach stretch traditional theories of political authority. 🤖

---

We examine how Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Rawls, and Nozick's frameworks handle three key questions:

• Who's accountable for algorithmic mistakes?
• Can people meaningfully consent to AI governance?
• How do we measure fairness in opaque systems? ⚖️

---

Classical social contract theories offer valuable insights but weren't built for algorithmic governance.

Our analysis reveals where these 350-year-old frameworks need updating for AI-driven decision-making. 📜

---

The stakes are real: AI systems increasingly determine who gets welfare benefits, police attention, job interviews, and public services.

We need governance frameworks that can handle both human and algorithmic authority. 🏛️

---

Results suggest hybrid approaches may be needed—combining procedural safeguards, participatory elements, and fairness principles adapted for algorithmic contexts.

No single theory handles all the challenges AI governance presents. 🔄

---

For policy practitioners and governance teams working on AI accountability: this framework offers a systematic way to think about legitimacy in algorithmic systems.
