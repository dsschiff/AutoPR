Can 350+ years of political philosophy help us govern AI? 🤔

Our new paper explores how social contract theories apply to algorithmic decision-making in welfare, policing, and hiring.

Published in AIES 2025

https://doi.org/10.1609/aies.v8i1.36575 👇

---

The challenge: AI systems now make decisions affecting millions, but weren't designed with democratic legitimacy in mind.

Transparency gaps, algorithmic bias, and global reach stretch traditional theories of political authority. 🤖

---

We examine how Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Rawls, and Nozick's frameworks handle:

• Who's accountable for algorithmic mistakes?
• Can people meaningfully consent to AI governance?
• How do we measure fairness in opaque systems? ⚖️

---

Classical social contract theories offer insights but weren't built for algorithmic governance.

Our analysis reveals where these frameworks need updating for AI-driven decision-making. 📜

---

Results suggest hybrid approaches may be needed—combining procedural safeguards, participatory elements, and fairness principles adapted for algorithmic contexts.

No single theory handles all AI governance challenges. 🔄

---

For policy practitioners working on AI accountability: this framework offers a systematic approach to legitimacy in algorithmic systems.
