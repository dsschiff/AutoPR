Can centuries-old political philosophy help us govern AI? Our new research published in AIES 2025 explores this question: https://doi.org/10.1609/aies.v8i1.36575

🤖 AI systems now make critical decisions in welfare distribution, policing, hiring, and public policy. But here's the challenge: these systems weren't designed with democratic legitimacy principles in mind.

📜 We examined how social contract theories from Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Rawls, and Nozick apply to algorithmic governance, focusing on three key areas:

✅ Accountability: Who owns responsibility for algorithmic mistakes?
✅ Consent: Can people meaningfully opt out of AI governance?
✅ Fairness: How do we measure justice in opaque, data-driven systems?

➤ Our analysis reveals that while classical theories offer valuable insights, they weren't built for the unique challenges of algorithmic authority—transparency gaps, bias, and global reach.

➤ Results suggest hybrid approaches may be needed, combining procedural safeguards with participatory elements and fairness principles adapted for AI contexts.

• No single theoretical framework handles all the governance challenges that AI systems present
• Policy practitioners need systematic approaches to legitimacy in algorithmic decision-making
• The stakes are real: AI increasingly determines access to benefits, services, and opportunities

For governance teams and policy practitioners working on AI accountability, this framework offers a structured way to think about legitimacy in algorithmic systems. [Add coauthors] [Add funder]
