AI education isn't one-size-fits-all. Our new research reveals how students' attitudes, interest, and knowledge shape their AI confidence—and identifies three distinct student profiles that need different educational approaches.

https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X24001437

• We surveyed 1,465 students across the US, UK, and Germany, published in Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence

• Using advanced modeling, we uncovered how cognitive (AI literacy), affective (interest/attitudes), and behavioral (usage) factors build AI self-efficacy

➤ Key findings suggest:
✅ Using AI tools (like ChatGPT) boosts interest
✅ Positive attitudes predict higher engagement  
✅ Interest acts as the bridge connecting attitudes, literacy, and confidence

➤ Three student profiles emerged:
🚀 AI Advocates (48%): Tech-savvy, confident, excited about AI
🤔 Cautious Critics (21%): Skeptical, low confidence, minimal AI use
⚖️ Pragmatic Observers (31%): Neutral attitudes, moderate interest

➤ Demographics matter: AI Advocates are mostly male STEM students, while Cautious Critics are overrepresented in humanities and predominantly female. Access to AI education varies widely—Critics report the least exposure.

• Implications for educators: AI programs need tailored approaches. Advocates need critical thinking about ethical AI. Critics need demystified, relevant applications. Observers need hands-on experiences to spark engagement.

Results suggest AI literacy isn't just about knowledge—it's about fostering interest and building confidence. Without addressing these factors, we risk leaving entire student groups behind in an AI-driven world.
