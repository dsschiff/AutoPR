AI education isn't one-size-fits-all 🎯

Our new study reveals how students' attitudes, interest, and knowledge shape their AI confidence—and identifies 3 distinct student profiles that need different approaches.

https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666920X24001437

🧵

---

We surveyed 1,465 students across the US, UK, and Germany to understand how cognitive (AI literacy), affective (interest/attitudes), and behavioral (usage) factors build AI self-efficacy.

Published in Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence 📊

---

Key findings suggest:

✅ Using AI tools (like ChatGPT) boosts interest
✅ Positive attitudes predict higher engagement
✅ Interest acts as the bridge connecting attitudes, literacy, and confidence

---

Using advanced modeling, we identified 3 student profiles:

🚀 AI Advocates (48%): Tech-savvy, confident, excited
🤔 Cautious Critics (21%): Skeptical, low confidence, minimal use
⚖️ Pragmatic Observers (31%): Neutral attitudes, moderate interest

[Attach image: img_1.jpg]

---

Demographics matter: AI Advocates are mostly male STEM students, while Cautious Critics are overrepresented in humanities and predominantly female.

Access to AI education varies widely—Critics report the least exposure 📈

---

Implications: AI programs need tailored approaches:

🚀 Advocates: Encourage critical thinking about ethical AI
🤔 Critics: Demystify AI, make it relevant to non-technical fields
⚖️ Observers: Use hands-on experiences to spark engagement

---

Results suggest AI literacy isn't just about knowledge—it's about fostering interest and building confidence. Without addressing these factors, we risk leaving entire student groups behind.
