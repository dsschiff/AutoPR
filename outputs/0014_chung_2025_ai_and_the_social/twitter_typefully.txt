Can AI governance align with democratic principles? 🤖

Our new paper examines how social contract theory—from Hobbes to Rawls—breaks down when applied to opaque algorithms that decide welfare, policing, and hiring.

Published in AIES 2025 👇🧵

https://doi.org/10.1609/aies.v8i1.36575

---

The challenge: AI systems now make critical decisions affecting millions, yet they're often opaque, unaccountable, and centrally controlled.

How does this square with democratic legitimacy? Are we creating an "algorithmic Leviathan"? ⚖️

---

We turned to social contract theory—the framework behind modern governance—to examine AI legitimacy.

Classical theorists like Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau assumed transparency and accountability. AI breaks these assumptions.

---

We identified three dimensions where AI governance fails:

✅ Procedural (transparency + participation)
✅ Moral-substantive (fairness + rights)
✅ Recognitional (including affected communities)

Current AI systems struggle on all fronts.

---

How do classical philosophers hold up against AI?

• Hobbes: Works for clear authority, fails with diffuse AI harms
• Locke: Requires "opt-out" options—impossible with pervasive algorithms
• Rousseau: Direct participation? Not at AI scale 📊

---

• Rawls: Assumes transparency for fairness—but algorithms lack clarity
• Nozick: Focuses on individual rights, misses collective harms like biased hiring models

Classical theories weren't built for opaque, global systems. 🌍

---

The solution? A hybrid social contract model that remixes classical ideas:

🔹 Hobbesian accountability
🔹 Lockean rights protection  
🔹 Rousseauian participation
🔹 Rawlsian fairness
🔹 Nozickian freedom from coercion

[Attach image: img_1.jpg]

---

Practical fixes for AI governance:

• Citizen assemblies for algorithmic oversight
• Real-time transparency dashboards
• Global cooperation (AI doesn't respect borders)
• Participatory regulation of biased systems 🛠️

---

We also integrate critical perspectives—feminist and intersectional critiques—to address systemic discrimination in both historical institutions and algorithmic systems.

AI governance needs pluralism, not one-size-fits-all ethics. 🌈

---

Why this matters: Algorithms aren't just tools—they're rulers mediating access to justice, jobs, and resources.
