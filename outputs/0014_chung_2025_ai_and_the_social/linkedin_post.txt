Can AI governance align with democratic principles? Our new research published in AIES 2025 examines this critical question: https://doi.org/10.1609/aies.v8i1.36575

AI systems now make decisions affecting millions—determining welfare eligibility, policing strategies, and hiring outcomes. Yet these systems are often opaque, unaccountable, and centrally controlled. How does this square with democratic legitimacy?

➤ We turned to social contract theory—the philosophical framework behind modern governance—to examine AI's legitimacy crisis. Classical theorists from Hobbes to Rawls assumed transparency and accountability. AI breaks these fundamental assumptions.

✅ Our analysis reveals three dimensions where current AI governance fails:
• Procedural legitimacy (transparency + participation)
• Moral-substantive legitimacy (fairness + rights protection)  
• Recognitional legitimacy (including affected communities)

➤ Classical philosophers struggle with AI realities:
• Hobbes works for clear authority but fails with diffuse AI harms
• Locke requires "opt-out" options—impossible with pervasive algorithms
• Rousseau's direct participation doesn't scale to automated systems
• Rawls assumes transparency for fairness—algorithms lack clarity
• Nozick focuses on individual rights, missing collective harms

✅ Our solution: A hybrid social contract model blending Hobbesian accountability, Lockean rights protection, Rousseauian participation, Rawlsian fairness, and Nozickian freedom from coercion.

Practical implications include citizen assemblies for algorithmic oversight, real-time transparency dashboards, and global cooperation frameworks. We also integrate critical perspectives to address systemic discrimination in both historical and algorithmic institutions.
