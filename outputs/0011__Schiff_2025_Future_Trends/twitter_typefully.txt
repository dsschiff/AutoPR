AI governance pulls us in 5 directions: centralized vs. decentralized control, robust vs. minimal enforcement, adaptive vs. enduring rules. How do we balance these tensions? 🤖

Our analysis draws lessons from aviation, climate policy & more 🧵

---

We examined 4 governance models to understand trade-offs:

✈️ Aviation: Centralized, safety-first, but costly
🌱 Organic food: Decentralized, private-driven, inconsistent
🌍 Climate: Shared, flexible, but weak enforcement
💻 Open-source: Collaborative, adaptive, but fragile

---

The current AI governance landscape shows clear fragmentation:

• EU centralizing with the AI Act
• US taking decentralized, sector-specific approach
• Monitoring ranges from strict compliance to voluntary audits
• Tech firms dominate decisions while public input is sidelined

---

The pace of AI development adds another layer of complexity. Generative AI's rapid emergence left policymakers scrambling.

A major crisis—like a deepfake election scandal—could force rapid regulatory responses. Public demand for accountability grows stronger daily.

---

Our analysis suggests hybrid models that combine:
• Centralized safety oversight (aviation-style)
• Decentralized innovation hubs (open-source approach)
• Broad societal goals (climate policy framework)

Experimentation across jurisdictions will be key.

---

Why this matters: AI governance will shape not just technology, but society itself. Done right, it could address inequality and climate impact. Done wrong, it risks entrenching power imbalances.

Published in Handbook on the Global Governance of AI.
